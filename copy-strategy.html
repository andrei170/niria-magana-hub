Audience register match

The copy speaks to three audiences with one voice. The voice does not change. The angle adjusts.

To the aspiring operator (cohort target)

  • Address: "You" (singular, direct)
  • Tone: "I've done this. Here's the playbook. You can do this too."
  • Avoid: "Empowerment" / "transformation" / "level-up" / "elevate" / "unlock"
  • Use: "Started", "ran", "shipped", "got the clients", "made the money", "ran the math"

To the warm-list customer (existing credit repair + tax client)

  • Address: "You" (with prior-relationship context - they've already paid for Yadi's services)
  • Tone: "You've seen how this works for you. Now here's how to run it yourself."
  • The warm-list voice acknowledges history: "When you came to me last year, I helped you clean up X. Now I'm teaching the playbook itself."

To Andrei (in outreach, the private bridge)

  • Address: "You" (operator-to-operator)
  • Tone: "We agreed to build this. I built it. Take a look. No pressure."
  • The outreach acknowledges the call-ghost pattern once, lightly, then moves on. The work is the substance.

  • "Elevate", "elevate your business"
  • "Transform", "transformative", "transformation"

  • "Guaranteed" (CROA + FTC red flag - the only "guarantee" language allowed is on credit-repair refund policies, never on income claims)

  • "Achieve your dreams", "live your dream life" (manifest-coach register, wrong)
  • "Your best self" (wellness-coach register, wrong)
  • Em-dashes anywhere except the sign-off "- Andrei" (the long-dash character is banned)

Tone calibration rules

1. Numbers are concrete and earned, never inflated

Wrong: "Make six figures from anywhere." Right: "Nikki cleared $40-50K in credit repair and $15K in taxes her first season."

The first reads like a course-bro pitch. The second reads like a fact you'd say at a kitchen table. The second sells harder to Yadi's actual audience.

2. The mentee is the hero, not the operator

Wrong: "I've helped my students change their lives." Right: "Nikki started while pregnant. Two jobs. One year later, she runs her own credit-repair business and quit one of those jobs."

The first centres Yadi. The second centres Nikki. The aspiring operator who reads this thinks "that could be me" - which is the conversion thought.

3. The math is the proof, but the math is rounded honest, not rounded up

Wrong: "Generate up to $100K in your first year." Right: "Nikki's first season ran $55K-$65K combined across credit repair and taxes. Year-one revenue depends on your effort, market, and execution."

The first is a class-action lawsuit waiting to happen. The second is a real ceiling, attributed to a real student, with the right qualifier baked in.

4. The regulatory reality is named, not hidden

The mentorship teaches a CROA-regulated business. Every public surface acknowledges this. Acknowledging it BUILDS credibility, doesn't hurt it - aspiring operators have read enough get-rich-quick credit-repair pitches that they're scanning for the compliance signal.

Voice: "I'll teach you how to run a CROA-compliant credit-repair business. The compliance module is one of the six pillars. Most courses skip it. We don't."

5. The faith / family / mindset bridge is light-touch, not preachy

Yadi's brand name is "Wealthy Beginnings" - the second-generation-wealth-for-the-family frame is built in. Lean into the family / generational framing without making the copy religious-coded (unless her IG bio confirms a faith register, which the visible surface doesn't yet show).

Voice: "Wealthy Beginnings isn't a name. It's what I'm building for my kids. The mentorship is for the women who want to build it for theirs too."

6. The call-ghost pattern is acknowledged once, in private, then dropped

In outreach to Yadi only - never in public copy: "We've kept agreeing to calls and the calls kept slipping. So I built it instead." One line. No apology. No "I miss you". Move on.

Sentence-level rules

  • Sentences under 22 words as the median. Long sentences only when the math requires them.
  • Active verbs over copular ("is", "are", "was") wherever the sentence allows.
  • Concrete nouns over abstract ("the cohort" > "the program", "the dispute letter" > "the strategy", "the $1,500 client" > "the customer").
  • "You" comes first when "you" is the subject. Don't bury the reader behind "I" / "we" / "the program".
  • No triple-adjective stacks ("powerful, transformative, life-changing"). Bans triple-adjective lists explicitly.
  • No "not just X, but Y" structure (overused AI-copy tell).
  • No "in today's world" / "in today's economy" openers (filler).

Headline / hero rules

For the landing page hero

  • Length: 7-12 words, two clauses max.
  • Format: Concrete promise + named differentiator. Example: "Build a credit-repair business the way Nikki did." (The proof point is the differentiator.)
  • Avoid: Hero questions ("Ready to change your life?"), pure-aspiration framing ("Live the life you deserve"), bro-pitch framing ("Make $10K/mo from your phone").

For the hub hero

  • Length: 10-16 words.
  • Format: Bridge acknowledgement + work-completed framing. Example: "Built from our June 3, 2025 conversation. The plan we agreed to, finally in writing."
  • Why: The hub is for Yadi specifically. The verbatim bridge IS the conversion lever.

If any check fails, rewrite before deploying.
